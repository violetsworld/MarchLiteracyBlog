Complex Flavors!
In "The Complex Matter of Text Complexity," Robert Rothman comments on research that points to declining complexity in high school texts despite steady or increasing complexity in entry level college texts. He explains how the selection process of choosing texts has also shifted from using texts written and tailored to a specific reading level, to choosing appropriately complex commercially available texts. Regardless of where the texts are sourced from, complexity is not a simple issue. While vocabulary and sentence structure are important, there are other factors such as layers of meaning or student familiarity with the content, that determine how complex a text is.
In the Appendix A of "Common Core State Standards for English Language Arts & Literacy in History/Social Studies, Science, and Technical Subjects," the authors cite much of the research summarized by Rothman, and also outline a three part model for measuring text complexity:

Quantitative:

Systematically scoring the length and frequency of words, length of sentences, syntax, and cohesion. Formulas like the Lexile Framework for Reading can place both readers and text on a complexity scale. Quantitative measurements are limited in their ability to factor content into their scoring.

Qualitative:

More easily measured by a human reader, factors that affect qualitative complexity are levels of meaning or purpose, structure, language conventionality and clarity, and knowledge demands.

Reader and Task:

In addition to using quantitative and qualitative metrics to rate the complexity of a text, educators are expected to use their professional judgement in order to choose an appropriate text for specific students and tasks.
